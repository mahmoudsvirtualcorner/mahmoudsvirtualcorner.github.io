Art Tools Referenced in Scientific Work

This page lists digital art tools that have been referenced in scientific and academic publications as part of workflows in visualization, design communication, or creative research. It aims to guide researchers and creators who wish to develop scientific work related to digital art, computational creativity, human-computer interaction (HCI), and visual communication.

For updates, please send me a message: zeidan.mahmoud@protonmail.com

Adobe Fresco

YearPublicationContext / Use in Research
2021 Practical Pigment Mixing for Digital Painting The study compared the proposed color pigment mixing model with state-of-the-art digital painting tools, evaluating its accuracy against real-world pigment mixing behavior.

Adobe Illustrator

YearPublicationContext / Use in Research
2025 Image Vectorization via Gradient Reconstruction Introduces a vectorization technique that reconstructs smooth color gradients from raster images, producing compact and accurate vector representations. Compared to Adobe Illustrator’s traditional vectorization—which relies solely on solid color fills and generates numerous complex paths—the proposed method achieves higher fidelity in tonal transitions and smoother shading.
2025 Single-Line Drawing Vectorization The study compared several design choices implemented in the proposed vectorization algorithm with Adobe Illustrator’s vector tracing functionality, analyzing differences in stroke continuity, curve fidelity, and handling of line junctions.
2024 Ciallo: GPU-Accelerated Rendering of Vector Brush Strokes This study compared the proposed GPU-accelerated vector brush rendering method against art tools in terms of raster stroke rendering, vector precision, and GPU acceleration performance.
2008 Diffusion Curves: A Vector Representation for Smooth-Shaded Images The proposed technique overcomes the limitations of traditional vector graphic curves by enabling smooth gradient value representation along and across curve boundaries, enhancing realism and tonal transitions in vector art.

ArtRage

YearPublicationContext / Use in Research
2015 Wetbrush: GPU-based 3D painting simulation at the bristle level The study highlights the visual outcomes of several virtual painting systems using identical stroke patterns. Traditional methods based on stamp maps or 2.5D height-field representations often fail to reproduce the natural irregularities and pigment blending observed in real brushwork. In contrast, the proposed system achieves realistic brush behavior with fine surface textures and physically consistent pigment variations within each stroke.
